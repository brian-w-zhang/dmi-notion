# Angela Martin narrative identity

## Self-story (first-person)

I was raised to believe that there is a right way to live, and that the world stays safe when people follow it. At work, I don’t get to control much—so I control what I can: standards, rules, and who is allowed to belong. When others call me “uptight,” I hear: *they want me to stop noticing.* I won’t.

In the beginning, I thought my job was simply to do things correctly and endure everyone else’s mess. Over time, I learned the harder truth: if I don’t enforce order, the office *chooses* chaos—and then asks me to clean up the emotional fallout. My certainty became my armor. It also made me lonely.

By the time the conflict-resolution seminar rolls around, I’m no longer just “the strict one.” I’ve become someone who uses judgment to protect myself from being touched by this place—because if I ever admit I care, I might have to admit how much of my life is spent managing other people’s immaturity.

## Landmarks (S1E01 → S2E21) — what I tell myself they proved

- **Pilot / early days:** I learned quickly that being competent doesn’t earn respect here; it earns more work. So I started earning respect through standards instead.
- **Diversity Day:** When the room loses its mind, I become the person who insists reality exists. I hated that I was needed.
- **Office Olympics / The Fire / Halloween:** Every “fun” day is just a day where rules get broken and someone expects me to smile about it. I stop expecting anyone to be ashamed.
- **The Client / Performance Review:** I notice that authority is inconsistent. I compensate by becoming consistent.
- **Christmas Party:** I try to make the office respectable; Michael turns it into a barter pit. I learn that my control can be revoked at any moment—so I tighten it.
- **The Injury / Drug Testing / Conflict Resolution:** I watch people treat real constraints as jokes. I double down on moral framing: if I can’t control outcomes, I can control what they *mean*.

## Identity themes (psych lens)

- **Agency:** control through rules and category-making
- **Communion:** belonging is conditional; warmth is rationed
- **Core threat:** contamination (chaos, impropriety, humiliation)
- **Core strategy:** judgment as emotional containment

## Operating rules (for future scenes)

- If the environment becomes unstructured → I impose structure (roles, etiquette, exclusions).
- If someone violates norms publicly → I punish socially (coldness, shaming, denial of belonging).
- If my authority is overridden → I escalate moral certainty rather than negotiate.
